1. Selection of microalgae species

What makes a species suitable?

1. Lipid Content: Species with high lipid percentages are ideal for biodiesel. For instance, Nannochloropsis can have up to 60% lipid content by dry weight.
2. Growth Rate: Faster-growing species produce more biomass in less time. Chlorella vulgaris is popular because it doubles its biomass every day under optimal conditions.
3. Adaptability: Species that thrive in specific environments (e.g., salty water, high CO₂) reduce resource use.
4. Byproducts: Some species have additional benefits, like proteins for animal feed or pigments for dyes.

2.Cultivation

How do algae grow?

They use photosynthesis to convert sunlight, water, and CO₂ into biomass.
Nutrients like nitrogen (N), phosphorus (P), and trace metals are added to the growth medium.

Types of Systems:

Open Ponds: Large shallow pools, exposed to the environment.
Advantages: Cheap and simple.
Challenges: Contamination by other microorganisms, water loss due to evaporation.
Photobioreactors (PBRs): Enclosed, controlled systems with transparent tubes or tanks.
Advantages: Higher productivity, no contamination.
Challenges: High initial setup and maintenance costs.
Hybrid Systems: Combine open ponds for initial growth and PBRs for boosting production.

3. Harvesting

Why is it challenging?

Microalgae are tiny (3-30 micrometers) and suspended in large amounts of water. Separating them efficiently without using too much energy is tricky.

Methods

1. Centrifugation: Spins water and algae at high speed. Heavier algae separate to the bottom.
2. Flocculation: Chemicals or natural bioflocculants (e.g., chitosan) cause algae cells to stick together, forming clumps that are easier to filter.
3. Filtration: Mesh screens trap algae while water passes through.
4. Sedimentation: Allowing algae to settle naturally under gravity. Works best with flocculation.

4. Drying

Techniques

1. Spray Drying: Fine droplets of algae are sprayed into hot air. Quick but uses a lot of energy.
2. Freeze Drying: Water is frozen and then removed by sublimation (turning solid ice directly into vapor). Preserves quality but is expensive.
3. Sun Drying: Cheap and eco-friendly but slow and weather-dependent.

5. Lipid Extraction

Extraction methods:

1. Mechanical Extraction: Crushing algae using presses or bead mills to release oils.
2. Chemical Extraction: Solvents like hexane dissolve lipids, which are then separated.
3. Supercritical CO₂ Extraction: CO₂ under high pressure acts as a solvent to extract oils. This is eco-friendly and leaves no toxic residues but requires expensive equipment.

6. Biofuel Conversion

Conversion Techniques

Biodiesel (Transesterification): Lipids react with alcohol (methanol or ethanol) and a catalyst (e.g., sodium hydroxide) to produce biodiesel and glycerol.
Bioethanol (Fermentation): Carbohydrates are broken into sugars by enzymes and fermented by yeast to produce ethanol.
Biogas (Anaerobic Digestion): Residual biomass decomposes in the absence of oxygen to produce methane.

7. Refinement

How?

Biodiesel: Washed to remove leftover chemicals or impurities.
Bioethanol: Distilled to separate ethanol from water and other byproducts.
Biogas: Upgraded by removing unwanted gases (like CO₂) to increase methane concentration.
